ADVANCED RAG · VISUAL EXPLAINER
TWO PATTERNS · FOUR USE CASES · TWELVE DIAGRAMS

Graph RAG &
Agentic RAG.

Two of the most consequential evolutions of standard RAG, shown as diagrams. Graph RAG retrieves over a web of connected entities. Agentic RAG retrieves in loops, deciding for itself. Both go where naïve vector search cannot follow.

I
Part one · The Knowledge Graph Approach

Graph RAG

RELATIONSHIPS MATTER

Standard RAG retrieves passages by similarity. It is excellent at "find the chunks that look like the question." It is helpless at "follow the chain of who-owns-whom from the parent company to the sanctioned ultimate beneficial owner." Graph RAG fixes that — by building a graph of entities and the relationships between them, then traversing it.

§ What is Graph RAG, in one paragraph

Graph RAG extracts entities (people, companies, products, accounts) and the relationships between them from your unstructured data, and stores them as a knowledge graph. At retrieval time, the system doesn't just fetch similar chunks — it traverses the graph, following relationships, and returns a connected subgraph as context.

This unlocks questions standard RAG cannot answer: "Which of our customers are connected to entities on the sanctions list through more than two intermediaries?" "What's the root cause network behind these eight related support tickets?"

Standard RAG → Graph RAG
Vector chunks→Entity + relations
Find similar→Traverse connected
Top-k passages→Subgraph of facts
Text only→Multi-hop reasoning
01
Graph RAG · Use Case · Banking · Financial Crime

Anti-Money Laundering & Beneficial Ownership

MULTI-HOP
REGULATED

The setting. A global bank's financial crime team must answer one question on every new corporate customer: "Is this entity, or anyone who ultimately controls it, on a sanctions list?" The answer lives across 14 sources — corporate filings, beneficial-ownership registries, news articles, leaked databases, internal KYC notes — and crucially, in relationships between them. Sanctioned actors hide three or four shell-company hops away.

STEP 01 · BUILD
01
Extract entities & relationships from documents

An LLM reads each document and pulls out structured triples — Person, Company, Country — and the verbs that connect them: owns, directs, incorporated_in, related_to.

Diagram 1.1 — Document → triples extraction → Structured knowledge
The shift

Standard RAG would store this document as 3 vector chunks. Graph RAG turns it into ~14 atomic facts, each pointing at named entities.

Why an LLM

Extracting "X owns Y at 85%" from prose is the kind of soft pattern-matching only an LLM does well at scale. The graph itself is built by AI.

STEP 02 · BUILD
02
Build the knowledge graph

Triples extracted from millions of documents are merged. Same-name entities are resolved (Petrov the person vs. Petrov the company). The graph stores nodes (entities) and edges (relationships), each tagged with source and date.

Diagram 1.2 — Triples → graph topology → Neo4j / graph store
Entity resolution

"K. Petrov" mentioned in 47 documents becomes one node. Aliases ("Konstantin P.", "K Petrov") merge through name + birthdate + ID matching.

Why graph DB

Neo4j, Neptune, or Memgraph store this natively. A JOIN on relational tables would melt at 4-hop queries.

STEP 03 · QUERY
03
Compliance officer asks; query is parsed

The system identifies the starting entity (the new customer, Acme) and the question type — a multi-hop ownership-chain traversal looking for sanctioned terminations.

Diagram 1.3 — Natural language → graph query → Cypher traversal
What's special

Standard RAG would search "Acme + sanctions" and find nothing because there's no direct mention. The graph query traverses 4 hops to find the link.

The pattern

This is a multi-hop traversal. The number of hops the regulator allows is configurable. Most banks set 4–5.

STEP 04 · TRAVERSE
04
Walk the graph, find the path

The graph engine starts at Acme and walks outward, following ownership and direction edges. It finds a 3-hop chain that lands on K. Petrov — flagged on the bank's internal sanctions list.

Diagram 1.4 — Traversal finds the hidden chain → Connected subgraph
Why this is impossible without a graph

The link doesn't appear in any single document. Three different filings, an article, and a list combine — only a graph traversal connects them.

Output

Not just a yes/no — the full subgraph: every entity in the chain, every edge, every source document. Auditable.

STEP 05 · GENERATE
05
LLM narrates the subgraph as a report

The retrieved subgraph and the original source documents go to the LLM. It produces an audit-ready report: the chain, the sources, the recommendation. Every claim cites a document and a graph edge.

Diagram 1.5 — Subgraph → narrated decision → Compliance officer
The deliverable

The output isn't a chatbot reply — it's a structured KYC memo the bank can place into the customer's file. Every claim has a document citation and a graph edge ID.

Auditor's view

An auditor can verify each hop independently — open the source, see the relationship, confirm the path. No "AI said so."

GRAPH RAG · USE CASE 1 · WRAP

What standard RAG would have missed entirely — a sanctioned ultimate beneficial owner three hops behind two shell companies — Graph RAG surfaced in seconds. Not by finding "similar text," but by finding a path through relationships.

02
Graph RAG · Use Case · Pharmaceuticals · R&D

Drug Repurposing & Mechanism Discovery

SCIENTIFIC
CROSS-DOMAIN

The setting. A pharma company's discovery team wants to know: "What approved drugs target genes in the same pathway as our new oncology candidate — and might therefore be repurposed?" The answer requires linking drugs → proteins → pathways → diseases → trials. No single paper says this. The connections are scattered across 30 million PubMed abstracts, 200,000 clinical-trial registrations, and proprietary internal research notes.

STEP 01 · BUILD
01
Build a biomedical knowledge graph

Entities and relationships are extracted from millions of papers and trial records. The graph has many node types — Drug, Protein, Pathway, Disease, Trial, Gene — and rich relationship vocabulary like inhibits, upregulates, indicated_for.

Diagram 2.1 — Multi-type biomedical graph schema → Knowledge base
Many node types

Unlike the banking case (mostly companies + people), pharma graphs have 10+ entity types and 50+ relationship types. The schema is the asset.

Built from literature

30M PubMed abstracts processed by extraction models pre-built for biomedicine (e.g. SciBERT) — Graph RAG sits on top.

STEP 02 · QUERY
02
Scientist poses a cross-domain question

A discovery scientist asks: "Find approved drugs that hit any protein in a pathway my candidate also affects — drugs that are currently approved for unrelated diseases." This is a pure graph query: 3 hops, mixing entity types.

Diagram 2.2 — Translating a research question into a path pattern → Multi-type Cypher
What's happening

The question chains 4 relationships. No vector search can encode this — it's structural, not semantic. Only a graph can express "two drugs share a pathway."

Pattern matching

The shape of the path matters: Drug → Protein → Pathway ← Protein ← Drug. Graphs are the only retrieval system that thinks in shapes.

STEP 03 · TRAVERSE
03
Traverse, collect candidates, rank

The graph engine returns every drug matching the pattern. Each candidate carries its full path — which protein, which pathway, which approved indication. The candidates are ranked by pathway overlap strength and number of supporting publications.

Diagram 2.3 — Candidates returned with full provenance paths → Ranked drug list
Each row is a path

Not "a similar document" — an actual chain of biology: Drug → its target → that target's pathway → another protein in the same pathway → another drug.

Confidence from evidence

Score reflects pathway overlap strength + number of supporting papers in the graph. The unfamiliar candidate (Mebendazole) is honestly ranked low.

STEP 04 · GENERATE
04
LLM writes the repurposing brief

The top candidates and their full evidence paths are handed to an LLM. It produces a literature brief: rationale for each candidate, supporting papers, what experiments to run next. Every claim is anchored in a graph path and at least one paper.

Diagram 2.4 — Final research brief with cited paths → Discovery scientist
The deliverable

Not just "here are 4 drugs" — an actionable brief with a testable hypothesis, the rationale, and the citations to defend it in front of a senior committee.

Two strengths combine

The graph supplies the structure (which paths exist). The LLM supplies the scientific narrative (why that path matters). Neither alone could do this.

GRAPH RAG · USE CASE 2 · WRAP

A repurposing hypothesis that would have taken a discovery scientist three weeks of literature review surfaced in twelve seconds — because Graph RAG can ask "what's connected to what," and "show me drugs that share a pathway" is a question naïve vector search literally cannot represent.

II
Part two · The Autonomous Loop

Agentic RAG

DECIDES FOR ITSELF

Standard RAG retrieves once and answers. Agentic RAG retrieves repeatedly — planning, searching, evaluating, deciding when to search again, deciding which tool to use, deciding when it's done. It behaves less like a search engine and more like a research analyst with a deadline.

§ What is Agentic RAG, in one paragraph

Agentic RAG places an LLM in the orchestrator's seat. It can break a complex question into sub-questions, pick from multiple tools (vector search, SQL, web search, calculator, the graph from Part I), execute them in sequence or in parallel, evaluate whether the answers it's getting are sufficient, and decide either to search again, switch tools, or conclude.

The defining feature isn't intelligence — it's iteration. The system loops until satisfied. That is what turns RAG from a query into a workflow.

Standard RAG → Agentic RAG
One retrieval→Many retrievals
One source→Many tools
Fixed plan→Adaptive plan
Always answers→Stops or asks
03
Agentic RAG · Use Case · B2B SaaS · Strategy

Competitive Intelligence & Deal Briefings

MULTI-SOURCE
ADAPTIVE

The setting. A B2B SaaS company's enterprise sales team has a £4M deal review tomorrow. The account exec needs a briefing covering: the customer's recent earnings narrative, their tech stack, the three competitors they evaluated us against, what those competitors said publicly this month, the prospect's open positions, and any executive moves. The answer lives across the web, the CRM, public filings, news, and the internal call-recording archive — and no single search resolves it.

STEP 01 · PLAN
01
Agent reads the brief, builds a plan

The account exec types one sentence. The agent — an LLM acting as orchestrator — decomposes it into ordered sub-tasks and chooses, for each, which tool it will use.

Diagram 3.1 — Question decomposed into a plan → Execution graph
The planning step

A standard RAG would have run one vector search on "Globex deal." An agent first decomposes, then routes each sub-question to the right tool.

Tool routing

News goes to a news API. Tech stack to BuiltWith. Past competitor mentions to the call-recording archive. The agent picks per sub-task.

STEP 02 · ACT
02
Execute tools in parallel

The agent fires off all five sub-tasks against their respective tools. Each tool returns a focused result. The agent collects them in a working memory.

Diagram 3.2 — Parallel tool execution & results → Working memory
The self-check

After tools return, the agent evaluates each result. This is the moment a standard RAG would have stopped — the agent doesn't.

Working memory

All results are kept in scratchpad memory and carried forward. The agent decides what to do with the gap.

STEP 03 · REFLECT
03
Detect the gap — pick a different tool

The agent notices Result 4 was thin on the competitor "Innotech." Rather than ignore it, the agent loops: re-routes the sub-question to a different tool (deep web search), broadens the query, and tries again.

Diagram 3.3 — The agent loops back → Filled gap
The loop is the point

Standard RAG would have produced a brief with a gap on Innotech and never noticed. The agent self-evaluates and goes back for more.

Bounded autonomy

Loops have a max-iteration limit (often 5). If still unresolved, the agent flags the gap honestly instead of fabricating.

STEP 04 · SYNTHESISE
04
Compose the deal briefing

With all five sub-tasks resolved, the agent writes a structured briefing — the kind a senior sales VP would compose, but in seconds — with every claim citing its tool source and the exact retrieval moment.

Diagram 3.4 — All five threads → one deal briefing → Account exec inbox
Multi-tool synthesis

The deliverable mixes facts from five different tools into one coherent narrative, with each fact traceable to its tool of origin.

The recommendation

Crucially the agent doesn't stop at facts — it reasons across them: "Innotech is cheap → pre-empt that in your pitch."

AGENTIC RAG · USE CASE 1 · WRAP

The account exec asked one sentence. The agent ran five tools, noticed a gap, looped back to fix it, then synthesised a sales-ready briefing — all in under a minute. The autonomy in the loop is what makes it agentic.

04
Agentic RAG · Use Case · Manufacturing · Supply Chain

Supplier Disruption & Resilience Triage

TIME-CRITICAL
MULTI-SYSTEM

The setting. An automotive manufacturer's supply chain control tower receives an alert: a typhoon has hit a region in southeast Asia containing 8 tier-2 suppliers. The procurement director needs, within an hour: which of our products are affected, how much inventory we have, what alternative suppliers we've qualified, what the financial exposure is, and which customer orders are at risk. The answer crosses ERP, supplier database, inventory system, financial model, CRM, and external news.

STEP 01 · TRIGGER
01
Event triggers the agent; it builds a triage plan

The disruption alert auto-starts the agent. Unlike the deal-briefing case, the question isn't asked by a human — it's triggered by an event. The agent must figure out what to do entirely from the alert payload.

Diagram 4.1 — Event-driven planning → Triage tree
The playbook

The agent doesn't invent its triage — it retrieves a vetted SOP from the company's library. The plan is grounded; only the data-gathering is autonomous.

Dependencies matter

Q5 can't run until Q1+Q2+Q4 complete. The agent builds a DAG (dependency graph) of the work, not a flat list.

STEP 02 · ACT
02
Run the DAG: parallel where possible, sequential where required

The agent fires off independent tasks in parallel and waits on dependent ones. As each tool returns, downstream tasks unblock. The execution flow shows what a human procurement analyst would normally orchestrate by hand.

Diagram 4.2 — Dependency-aware execution → Filled answers
Parallel + dependent

Q1 and Q2 run together. Q3 and Q4 wait for Q1's BOM data. Q5 waits for everything. The agent orchestrates the DAG automatically.

Mid-run confidence checks

After Q3 returns alternates, the agent checks confidence. Above 70% — proceed. Below — it would have escalated to a human.

STEP 03 · RECOMMEND
03
Compose findings & propose actions

The agent synthesises all five answers into a triage report for the procurement director — and goes one step further: it proposes specific actions, ranked by impact, that a human can approve with one click.

Diagram 4.3 — Findings + recommended actions → Procurement director
Beyond reporting

The agent doesn't just answer — it proposes specific actions: issue this PO, draft these emails, open this RFQ.

Human-in-the-loop, always

The agent never executes a write — a procurement director must approve each action. Autonomy in research, oversight in action.

AGENTIC RAG · USE CASE 2 · WRAP

An alert that would have triggered a four-hour analyst scramble produced a triage report and three approved actions in 45 seconds — because an agent, working from a playbook, can run six systems in dependency order, evaluate confidence as it goes, and synthesise like a junior analyst with no caffeine and no calendar.

§ When to reach for which

Two patterns. Two very different jobs.

Graph RAG · Picks the right job

When the answer is a path through relationships.

  • Multi-hop questions ("who controls whom, four hops out")
  • Cross-domain reasoning (drugs ↔ proteins ↔ pathways ↔ diseases)
  • Knowledge that lives between documents, not inside any one
  • Auditable claims with full provenance paths
  • Stable, structured domains — banking, biomedicine, fraud, regulation
Agentic RAG · Picks the right job

When the answer requires deciding, iterating, judging.

  • Multi-source questions that mix tools (web + CRM + warehouse + API)
  • Open-ended research where the plan must adapt to findings
  • Event-driven triage with bounded autonomy and human approval
  • Workflows where stopping criteria matter as much as the answer
  • Fast-moving domains — sales, supply chain, ops, intelligence
